Chapter 03 · Idea Stage

Stop building. Start validating.

Every startup founder starts from the same place: a problem they can't stop thinking about. This is the phase where idea meets reality — and where startup success in 2026 requires the discipline of not building until the evidence justifies it.

A working prototype is easy to mistake as concrete evidence that you're solving a real problem. It isn't. The conversations are. — Founder's Playbook, Ch. 3

The goal of this stage

Research-oriented validation: assembling solid evidence that a real problem exists, and that your proposed solution effectively addresses it, before committing resources to building.

Three questions you must answer

  1. Is the problem real, specific, and frequent enough to build around?
  2. Does your solution address the actual problem — not the one you originally assumed?
  3. Do you have enough signal to justify building?
Exercise Work with Claude to sharpen your problem statement until it's a testable hypothesis. "Contract review takes too long" is not testable. "In-house legal teams at mid-market companies spend 3+ days per cycle because redlines live across email threads" is.
Note Using Claude as a structured devil's advocate is a core use case at every stage of the AI startup life cycle, not just here.
Chapter 03 · Idea Stage

Mistaking building for validating

When technical blockers are lifted, an impassioned founder risks skipping the most important work of the startup journey: validating that their idea is genuinely a solution that people need.

42%
of startups failed because they built something nobody wanted — and that was before agentic coding made shipping a prototype an afternoon's work.

Choosing the right Claude surface

AI makes it easier to ship faster, automate workflows, and operate at scale — but the surface you use matters.

If the task is… Reach for Why
A question, a rewrite, a quick brainstorm Chat Fast, conversational, no setup
Research, analysis, or a finished document built from your files Cowork Folder access, connectors, scheduled runs
Writing, testing, or shipping software Code Codebase access, diffs, git, dev environments

The three share the same Claude underneath; what changes is the workspace around it.
